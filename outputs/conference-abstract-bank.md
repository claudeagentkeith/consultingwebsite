### 250-word abstract — Lifecycle integration for combination products

> **Title:** Where combination-product programs actually stall: a lifecycle view of 21 CFR Part 4 in practice
>
> **Track:** Regulatory & Quality Strategy / Combination Products
>
> Combination-product programs rarely stall on 21 CFR Part 4 itself. They stall on the interfaces — between device design controls and drug CGMP, between primary mode of action analysis and submission strategy, and between change-control logic and the constituent the change actually touches. This session walks the three predictable stall points in combination-product programs and the architectural decisions that resolve each.
>
> The session covers: (1) how to architect the streamlined CGMP framework under §4.4(a) so that the additional specified provisions of Part 211 or Part 820 are integrated into one quality system rather than running as a parallel shadow system; (2) where primary-mode-of-action determinations under 21 CFR 3.2(m) most commonly diverge from the FDA Office of Combination Products' view, and what to do about it before substantial engineering commits; (3) how to structure change control across the device-drug interface so that a change touching one constituent triggers evaluation of effects on the other.
>
> Attendees will leave with a six-step framework for standing up streamlined CGMP, a four-question evaluation pattern for cross-interface changes, and the language to advocate for an integrated quality system inside their organization.
>
> **Learning objectives:**
> 1. Identify the three structural stall points in combination-product programs.
> 2. Apply the streamlined CGMP framework under 21 CFR 4.4(a) to a real program.
> 3. Architect change control that spans device design controls and drug CGMP.

### 500-word abstract — same topic, fuller version

> **Title:** Where combination-product programs actually stall: a lifecycle view of 21 CFR Part 4 in practice
>
> **Track:** Regulatory & Quality Strategy / Combination Products
>
> 21 CFR Part 4 is one of the shortest regulations in the medical-product world. Subpart A — the streamlined CGMP framework that governs how a combination product can comply with both the device QSR and drug CGMP through one integrated system — runs to a few pages. The brevity is deceptive. Part 4 sits at the most architecturally complex intersection in regulated-product development: the place where two regulatory frameworks, two quality vocabularies, two inspection cadences, and two engineering disciplines have to be integrated into a single coherent system that survives a coordinated FDA inspection.
>
> Combination-product programs rarely stall on the regulation. They stall in three predictable places. This session walks each in detail with reference to specific CFR sections and to the architectural decisions practitioner teams should make.
>
> Stall 1 — Constituent classification and primary mode of action. PMOA under 21 CFR 3.2(m) anchors lead-center jurisdiction, submission pathway, and which framework anchors the streamlined CGMP system. Programs that defer the PMOA argument or make it as a regulatory-affairs deliverable rather than an architectural decision pay the cost twice. The session covers when to validate PMOA through pre-IND or pre-submission meetings.
>
> Stall 2 — Streamlined CGMP integration. Section 4.4(a) lets manufacturers anchor on Part 820 (with the additional specified Part 211 provisions of §4.4(b)(1)) or anchor on Part 211 (with the additional specified Part 820 provisions of §4.4(b)(2)). Two anti-patterns produce inspection findings: the shadow system and the blended system without anchor. The session walks the architectural pattern that does work.
>
> Stall 3 — Change control across the device-drug interface. A change-control system that handles device changes correctly under 820.30(i) and drug changes correctly under Part 211 can still be non-compliant under Part 4 if a change touching the device but affecting the drug is not evaluated under both frameworks. The session walks the single change-control board pattern with dual-framework evaluation logic.
>
> Attendees will leave with: a six-step practitioner framework for standing up streamlined CGMP, a four-question evaluation pattern for cross-interface changes, the regulatory citations needed to advocate for an integrated quality system internally, and a clear view of where the next inspection or submission is most likely to surface gaps.
>
> **Learning objectives:**
> 1. Identify the three structural stall points in combination-product programs and where they typically appear in the program timeline.
> 2. Apply the streamlined CGMP framework under 21 CFR 4.4(a) including the §4.4(b) specified-provision integration.
> 3. Architect a change-control system that spans device design controls (820.30(i)) and drug CGMP (Part 211) under one integrated board.

### 250-word abstract — Manufacturing transfer readiness

> **Title:** The 12 things most medical-device teams miss before manufacturing transfer
>
> **Track:** Design for Manufacturing / Quality & Process
>
> Design transfer under 21 CFR 820.30(h) is one of the most expensive seams in medical-device development. The regulation is short — a single paragraph. The readiness work behind a successful transfer is the largest single integration effort in a regulated-product program, covering design output completeness, process validation under 820.75, supplier qualification under 820.50, and inspection posture across the design history file and device master record.
>
> This session walks twelve specific gaps practitioner teams should close before transfer kicks off. The gaps are organized into three clusters: design output package (BOM completeness, tolerance vs. process capability, inspection criteria, DFMEA-PFMEA cross-reference); process readiness (IQ/OQ/PQ scope, DMR construction, test method validation, representative-build batches); and supplier and QMS readiness (820.50 supplier qualification, transfer-phase change control, incremental DHF construction, inspection-readiness rehearsal).
>
> Each gap is tied to a specific CFR or ISO 13485 section, illustrated with a representative pattern from regulated-product programs, and paired with the architectural fix that closes it before transfer rather than during PQ execution.
>
> Attendees will leave with a 12-point readiness checklist, the regulatory citations supporting each item, and a working version of the manufacturing-engagement-during-design-output pattern that prevents most late-stage DFM findings.
>
> **Learning objectives:**
> 1. Identify the 12 specific gaps that produce expensive late discoveries during manufacturing transfer.
> 2. Apply the cross-FMEA review pattern to surface design-process integration gaps before transfer.
> 3. Architect a manufacturing readiness lane that runs in parallel with verification.

### 250-word abstract — Design controls that survive audit

> **Title:** Design controls that survive audit: architecting a 21 CFR 820.30 system that holds up under coordinated inspection
>
> **Track:** Quality / Compliance Strategy
>
> Design controls under 21 CFR 820.30 are not a paperwork exercise. The regulation lists eight elements plus the design history file. The architectural decisions behind a design-controls system that holds up under FDA inspection are not. This session walks the four common failure patterns and the six-property architecture that resists them.
>
> The four failure patterns surface as Form 483 observations under 820.30(c), (f), (g), (i), or (j): the retroactively-assembled DHF; the traceability matrix that doesn't close; the risk file as a parallel deliverable; and the change-control system that scopes too narrowly. Each has a structural cause that programs can recognize from its shape long before an inspector arrives.
>
> The six-property architecture that resists these patterns: one traceability spine, approval-time discipline on inputs, risk integration into requirements (not parallel to them), cross-disciplinary design reviews, tight change control with broad impact assessment, and incremental DHF construction. Each property is tied to specific CFR sections and to the architectural pattern that implements it.
>
> The session also covers the ISO 13485:2016 §7.3 overlay and the FDA's 2024 final rule incorporating ISO 13485 by reference (the QMSR transition).
>
> Attendees will leave with a structural diagnostic for their own design-controls posture and the regulatory citations needed to advocate for the architectural fixes inside their organization.
>
> **Learning objectives:**
> 1. Identify the four common 820.30 failure patterns from their structural shape.
> 2. Apply the six-property architecture as a diagnostic for an existing design-controls system.
> 3. Plan the QMSR transition (FDA's 2024 final rule incorporating ISO 13485:2016) for an existing 820.30-anchored QMS.

### 250-word abstract — User research to verifiable design inputs

> **Title:** From user research to verifiable design inputs: a lifecycle framework integrating IEC 62366-1:2015 and 21 CFR 820.30(c)
>
> **Track:** Human Factors / Usability Engineering
>
> User research produces narrative artifacts: a use specification per IEC 62366-1:2015 §5, a use-scenario inventory, a list of pain points, an inventory of use environments. Design controls under 21 CFR 820.30(c) require the opposite — discrete, measurable design inputs that pass the test of being appropriate to the device and addressing the needs of the user and patient.
>
> Between the narrative artifacts of user research and the verifiable statements of design inputs, there is a translation step. This session walks a five-stage framework for that translation step: use specification (§5), user needs and use scenarios, design inputs with verification approach, design outputs and traceability, verification and validation.
>
> The translation step is where most programs either invent the gap that haunts them later or close it cleanly. The session works through a representative example — a narrative user need translated into two verifiable design inputs with verification approach and acceptance criteria — and walks the bidirectional traceability that connects user research to design validation under 820.30(g).
>
> Attendees will leave with a working method for the user-research-to-design-input translation, the IEC 62366-1:2015 §6.2 hazardous-use-scenario integration with the ISO 14971 risk file, and a clear view of how the translation step prevents the most common Form 483 finding under 820.30(c).
>
> **Learning objectives:**
> 1. Apply the five-stage framework to translate user-research narrative into verifiable design inputs.
> 2. Integrate hazardous use scenarios per IEC 62366-1:2015 §6.2 with the ISO 14971 risk file.
> 3. Architect bidirectional traceability connecting user research to design validation.
